## Outcome

Write a specific, evidence-backed article that gives the reader a useful idea, model, or changed understanding. It may inspire, but inspiration must be earned by the argument.

## Do not begin with prose

Before drafting, establish:

- intended reader
- reader's current belief or behavior
- one arguable thesis
- why the author is positioned to make the argument
- evidence, mechanism, or lived material
- strongest counterargument
- practical consequence
- what the article will deliberately exclude

When the article must earn attention, also read `audience-value-loop.md` and establish:

- the strongest commodity version of the argument
- the author's evidence, access, demonstration, synthesis, or judgment advantage
- the audience delta that survives after biography, branding, and polished phrasing are removed

If the thesis is generic, do not solve the problem only by generating sharper wording or a more provocative opinion. First look for a supported knowledge advantage. Generate materially different angles from the available evidence and choose one. Do not merge all angles into a broad article.

If no supported advantage exists, narrow the promise, gather new evidence, request author input, or return `BLOCKED`. Do not invent a taxonomy, proprietary-sounding term, personal story, or organizational bet to simulate originality.

## Quality references

Named writers such as swyx, Theo Browne, or Garry Tan may be used only as references for transferable craft:

- doing the work before explaining it
- clear mental models
- concrete technical receipts
- direct opinions with self-awareness
- fair treatment of the previous conventional wisdom
- human stakes beneath technical change
- ambition grounded in mechanism
- endings that expand naturally from evidence

Do not imitate voice, cadence, catchphrases, personal stories, or signature structures.

## Research handoff

The research loop and source-integrity audit should provide:

- sourced facts
- examples
- relevant history
- current context
- contradictions
- uncertainty
- claims that are safe to make
- claims that require qualification
- source classes, incentives, and independence risks
- marketing, affiliate, PR, SEO, AEO, or GEO claims that must not be presented as neutral validation

The article loop must not invent personal experience, customer stories, quotes, metrics, or vulnerability.

## Distribution is not the thesis

SEO, AEO, GEO, demand generation, and campaign goals may shape packaging and distribution, but they must not manufacture the argument.

When discoverability is requested:

1. Finish the reader value, thesis, evidence, and structure first.
2. Apply current technical and structural discovery practices afterward.
3. Keep optimization edits from changing factual meaning or voice.
4. Reject filler sections, fake FAQs, repetitive definitions, keyword coverage paragraphs, citation decoration, and inflated length.
5. Do not promise rankings, citations, mentions, or answer-engine inclusion.
6. Label engine-specific tactics as experimental unless current official evidence supports them.

A useful article should still deserve to exist if search and answer engines sent it no traffic.

## Argument architecture

Build a progression, not a content checklist.

A useful default shape is:

1. Concrete tension, observation, or problem
2. The belief that makes the problem hard to see
3. Evidence or mechanism that changes the interpretation
4. Consequences for the reader
5. Counterargument or boundary
6. Practical model or action
7. Earned ending

Adapt the shape to the material. Do not force a formula.

Every section must do at least one job:

- advance the thesis
- provide evidence
- explain mechanism
- make stakes concrete
- answer an objection
- give a usable model
- change the scale of the conclusion

Remove sections that only restate the premise.

## Drafting rules

- Use concrete nouns and verbs.
- Prefer examples over abstract claims.
- Vary sentence and paragraph length naturally.
- Use headings only when they help navigation.
- Avoid excessive bullets in narrative writing.
- Do not fabricate certainty.
- Do not address every possible audience.
- Do not begin with generic trend framing.
- Do not copy vendor positioning or search-result consensus into the author voice.
- Separate promotional intent from factual claims and disclose affiliation when relevant.
- Do not make every paragraph a symmetrical list.
- Avoid em dashes when the user's style forbids them.
- Avoid using em dashes as automatic rhythm even when allowed.

## Inspiring without slop

An ending can be ambitious when the article has demonstrated:

- a real shift in constraints
- a newly available capability
- a responsibility the reader now holds
- a practical path that did not previously exist

Do not add motivational language to compensate for a weak argument.

Avoid endings that merely recap headings, say the future is here, or tell the reader to embrace change.

## Revision cycle

1. Read only for argument.
2. Repair unsupported or missing logic.
3. Read only for evidence and examples.
4. Repair factual or concrete gaps.
5. Read only for reader value and pacing.
6. Hand to editorial loop.
7. Recheck claims after editorial changes.

## Return criteria

Return `PASS` when the draft has one defensible thesis, real evidence or mechanism, a coherent progression, a fair boundary, concrete reader value, an earned ending, and a supported audience delta when the artifact must earn attention. Report whether the draft is `ARTIFACT VERIFIED` or its intended reader outcome was actually `OUTCOME VALIDATED`.

Return `REWORK_REQUIRED` when the argument or evidence remains generic.
Return `BLOCKED` when necessary evidence or author context is unavailable.
Return `NEEDS_HUMAN_JUDGMENT` when two strong angles remain and the choice depends on the author's conviction or voice.
